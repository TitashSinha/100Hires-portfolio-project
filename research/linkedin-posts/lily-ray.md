Google just launched new communications about its approach to generative AI, covering the impact of AI on jobs, the environment, misinformation, and the other ways AI is reshaping society.

Hat tip to Marie Haynes for sharing and highlighting some key information that publishers and content creators have been demanding for a long time.

And most importantly:

I will certainly be watching this space closely. Personally, I believe _all_ content creators whose work is used in AI training and/or grounding should be compensated according to how frequently their content is used.

A couple years back, the website RickSteves.com was making the rounds in SEO discussions, and I also shared some data in my talks and social posts about its growth.

I just noticed his site is continuing to surge in organic & AI search visibility, with a huge increase in AI Overview citations since the May core update.

To me, the site/brand is the ultimate embodiment of E-E-A-T:

Experience: Rick has spent ~4 months a year in Europe for decades, walking the routes and updating guidebooks annually

Expertise: The site provides thousands of pages of genuinely useful, free, experience-driven content, instead of thin affiliate clickbait or articles overly-optimized for search

Authoritativeness: Rick has made himself the go-to guy for American travel in Europe for ~30 years. He's had a PBS series since 2000, a long-running public radio show, a tour company, and annually updated guidebooks.

Trustworthiness: He tells you what's overrated, overpriced, or skippable. The honesty is the product. He makes money by selling books and tours, so his content is free from affiliate influence.

He also has a diversified business model, which I believe supports his growth in organic & AI search:

- Guidebooks
- PBS TV series
- Guided tours
- Website as a free content hub
- Travel gear and accessories

His content strategy is also diversified:

- TV travel series
- YouTube (free archive of full TV library)
- TikTok
- Radio/podcast (public radio call-in show)
- Rick Steves community forum (genuine UGC)

The AI Overviews that cite his brand often cite his own website, plus his YouTube AND TikTok pages too.

This is a brand that is set up for success. Using what I've been calling the "E-E-A-T Method" sets a brand up for success for years.

## Post 3

**Date:** June 20, 2026
**URL:** https://www.linkedin.com/posts/lily-ray-44755615_for-the-last-couple-of-years-the-easiest-share-7473139501825966080-FnsE/

For the last couple of years, the easiest GEO hack in the book was to publish a "best [category]" listicle and rank yourself #1. It worked to influence AI answers, and in many cases, it still does.

But it also might be handing the win to your competitors now, at least on AI Overviews.

I pulled 100 B2B "best [category]" queries from Google's AI Overviews across three dates between April and June, and separated two of the main AI search success metrics: getting cited as a source vs. actually getting recommended in the answer. Turns out those are very different outcomes.

When a brand's own self-promotional listicle got cited, that brand was left out of the actual recommendation 69% of the time. And the recommendations went to the established competitors they'd listed in their own article.

So you write a listicle, name your competitors, call yourself the best, and Google cites you as the source while recommending everyone else on your list. You just inadvertently made the case for your competition.

Google seems to have decoupled what it cites from who it recommends, and the recommendations appear to be anchored to actual authority rather than how many times you've called yourself #1.

If AI citations are your main KPI, I'd reconsider. I'd argue that being recommended is much more valuable.

> **Research note:** Ray presents data showing self-promotional listicles get cited as sources while competitors get recommended 69% of the time. AI recommendations are anchored to actual authority, not self-reported rankings.
